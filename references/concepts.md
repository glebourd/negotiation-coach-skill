# Core Negotiation Concepts

These frameworks are drawn from the canon of negotiation research and practice -- the same material taught in business school negotiation courses at Harvard, Wharton, Stanford, and elsewhere. The foundational work comes from Fisher and Ury (*Getting to Yes*), Bazerman and Neale (*Negotiating Rationally*), Raiffa (*The Art and Science of Negotiation*), and Thompson (*The Mind and Heart of the Negotiator*). These aren't academic abstractions -- they're the most battle-tested frameworks in the field.

---

## Key Variables

**BATNA** (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement) — What you do if no deal is reached. Source of power. Rarely revealed. Should be actively strengthened before negotiating, not just identified. Concept originated by Fisher and Ury -- arguably the single most important idea in negotiation theory.

**Reservation Price (RP)** — Your walk-away point. Derived from BATNA but adjusted for relationship value, switching costs, and strategic importance. Never reveal -- not even post-agreement. Revealing post-agreement destroys future leverage and signals your floor for every future interaction.

**Aspiration Point** — Realistic but optimistic target. Set before entering. Research consistently shows that negotiators with higher aspirations achieve better outcomes. Don't adjust downward under pressure -- that's precisely when the aspiration point matters most.

**ZOPA** (Zone of Possible Agreement) — The range between both parties' reservation prices. Positive ZOPA = a deal is possible. Must explore the full ZOPA before concluding impasse -- most failed negotiations never tested the full range.

## Anchoring

Research by Tversky and Kahneman demonstrated that first numbers powerfully anchor subsequent judgments -- even when the number is arbitrary. In negotiation, this means:

- First offers anchor final agreements strongly, often explaining 50%+ of outcome variance
- Make the first offer when prepared -- don't cede anchoring by default
- If counterpart anchors badly: re-anchor immediately with justification. Don't negotiate from their number. The moment you start adjusting their anchor, you've lost your frame.
- If counterpart gives a good first offer: never accept outright. Ask for concessions -- this increases satisfaction for both parties (the "winner's curse" of immediate acceptance)

---

## Concessions

- Leave room in your opening for concessions -- don't open at your RP
- Tie each concession to a reason ("I can move on this because...")
- Shrink concession size as you approach your target -- this signals you're nearing your limit
- Concessions must be bilateral -- unilateral conceding signals weakness and invites further demands
- Make concessions conditional ("If you can do X, I can do Y")
- Distinguish substantive concessions from procedural gestures (offering to take a break, move to email, restructure the agenda)

## Negotiation Types

The most consequential diagnosis in any negotiation. Getting this wrong means applying the wrong strategy -- and the wrong strategy executed well is worse than no strategy at all.

**Integrative** — Multiple issues, different priorities across issues, trades possible. Strategy: surface interests (not positions), expand the pie through trades, then claim your share. MESOs are powerful here. Fisher and Ury's "principled negotiation" was designed for this context.

**Distributive** — Single issue, fixed sum. Strategy: anchor strongly, make strategic concessions, manage emotion, protect information. The most common strategic error is applying integrative tactics (sharing interests, building rapport) to a distributive context -- where the counterpart can use your openness against you.

**Mixed** — Most real-world negotiations. Some issues are distributive, others are integrative. The skill is diagnosing each issue independently and applying the right approach to each.

**Diagnosis is the prerequisite.** Before choosing any tactic, name the type and explain why.

## Interests vs. Positions

Fisher and Ury's core insight: positions are what people say they want ("I need $X"). Interests are why they want it ("I need financial security," "I need to feel respected," "I need predictability for my team").

- Negotiating positions against each other creates deadlock
- Negotiating interests against each other creates trades
- The question "Why is that important to you?" unlocks more value than any tactical move
- Hidden interests (unstated constraints, ego, precedent concerns, third-party relationships) often matter more than stated ones

---

## MESOs (Multiple Equivalent Simultaneous Offers)

A technique from Bazerman's research that solves two problems at once: revealing counterpart priorities and signaling cooperation.

- Present 2-3 packages simultaneously, each worth roughly the same to you
- Counterpart's choice reveals their priorities without requiring direct disclosure
- Signals flexibility and cooperation -- especially powerful after an impasse or trust breakdown
- Most effective combined with async (email) format -- gives both sides evaluation time
- Deploy when direct questioning has hit resistance or when trust needs rebuilding

---

## Scoring Systems

- Assign point values to issues and outcomes across the full issue set
- Enables MESO construction -- packages with equivalent total scores but different compositions
- Forces explicit prioritization before entering -- many negotiators discover their own priorities aren't clear until they try to weight them numerically
- Quantifies RP and aspiration in multi-issue contexts where "walk-away point" isn't a single number

---

## Breaks and Impasse

One of the most underused and highest-impact tactical moves. Taking a break is precision, not weakness.

- Forces more deliberate proposals from both sides
- Prevents reactive concessions driven by emotional pressure
- Especially effective when: real-time math is needed, trust has eroded, offers have stalled, or you need to consult with someone
- Async (email) after a break allows MESO presentation with proper evaluation time
- "Let me take some time to think about this and get back to you" is always acceptable

---

## Trust

Research by Malhotra and Bazerman (*Negotiation Genius*) on trust dynamics:

- Impasses are more likely with low-trust counterparts -- not because deals aren't possible, but because neither side shares enough information to find them
- Trust rebuilders: demonstrate similarity, mirror counterpart's language, reciprocate concession gestures, follow through on small commitments
- Don't gloat after wins -- poisons the ongoing relationship and creates adversaries for future interactions
- Hidden parties (bosses, boards, partners, advisors) can constrain counterpart flexibility in ways you can't see -- always ask who else has input on the decision

---

## Decision Rule

Only accept deals better than your BATNA -- regardless of time pressure, social pressure, sunk costs, or emotional investment. This is the one rule that, if followed consistently, prevents every catastrophic negotiation outcome.
