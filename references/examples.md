# Worked Examples

These examples demonstrate how the coaching system works in practice — scoring, triage decisions, and analysis patterns. They are generic and illustrative, not case-specific.

---

## Example 1: Salary Negotiation — Scored Simulation Round

**Context:** Developing negotiator, job offer negotiation. Offered $130k base. Market data supports $145-165k. User's target is $155k.

**User's opening move:**
"Thank you so much for the offer. I was hoping we could discuss the compensation. I was thinking something more in the $145k range would be fair."

**Coach Assessment:**

| Dimension | Score | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Preparation | 3 | Has market data and a target. BATNA not stated. |
| Positioning | 2 | Anchor at $145k is below target ($155k). "Hoping" and "fair" soften the ask. Thanking before asking creates a concession-ready frame. |
| Flexibility | N/A | First move — can't assess yet. |
| Composure | 3 | Delivered the ask. Didn't apologize. But hedging language suggests discomfort. |
| Outcome | N/A | Too early. |

**Coaching feedback (directness level 4):**
"You made the ask, which many people don't — that's real. But two things cost you here. First, your anchor of $145k is below your actual target of $155k. You've given yourself almost no room to negotiate down and still land where you want. Anchor at $165k — that's the top of the market range, and you can justify it with the data you have. Second, the words 'hoping' and 'fair' soften your position. You're not hoping — you're stating what the market supports. Try: 'Based on my research, the market range for this role at this level is $145-165k. Given my background in [specific value], I'm targeting $165k.' No apology. No hope. Just a justified number."

## Example 2: Triage Decision After Simulation

**Data:** After 3 simulation rounds, scores are:
- Preparation: 4, 4, 4 (consistently strong)
- Positioning: 3, 2, 2 (declining)
- Flexibility: 3, 3, 3 (flat)
- Composure: 2, 2, 1 (declining)
- Outcome: 3, 2, 2 (declining)

**Triage diagnosis:**
"Your preparation is consistently strong — you're doing the analytical work. But your Positioning and Composure are both declining, and that's dragging your Outcomes down. Here's what I think is happening: you're preparing well but then not executing under pressure. Your Composure drop from 2 to 1 in the last round suggests the pressure layers are exposing an execution gap, not a knowledge gap.

This is a psychological pattern, not a strategic one. More preparation won't fix it. What will: (1) simulate silence drills to build comfort with pauses, (2) script your first 2-3 sentences exactly so you don't have to improvise under pressure, (3) practice the moment between hearing their counter and responding — that 5-second gap is where your composure breaks."

**Coaching strategy update:**
- Primary bottleneck: Composure → execution under pressure
- Approach: Silence drills + scripted openings + deliberate pause practice
- Pivot if: Composure reaches 3+ but Positioning stays at 2

## Example 3: Debrief — Contract Negotiation

**Context:** User negotiated a consulting contract. Had a moderate BATNA (other client interest, not confirmed). Aspiration was $15k/month retainer. RP was $10k. Outcome: $11.5k with a 6-month minimum.

**Self-assessment:** "I think it went okay. I got above my minimum."

**Coach assessment:**
"You're right that you beat your RP — $11.5k is above $10k. But 'above my minimum' is not the right benchmark. Your aspiration was $15k, and the ZOPA likely extended to at least $13k based on how quickly they accepted $11.5k.

Three things happened here. First, you anchored at $13k instead of $15k — your own aspiration should have been the floor of your anchor, not the ceiling. Second, when they countered at $10.5k, you moved immediately to $11.5k without asking what would make $13k work for them. That's a $1.5k unilateral concession in one move. Third, they accepted $11.5k without any hesitation — which tells me they would have gone higher.

Your deal quality is 'above RP but well below what was available.' The lesson: anchor at or above aspiration, not below it. And when they counter, ask a question before conceding — 'What would need to be true for [your number] to work?'"

**Scorecard:**
- Preparation: 3 (BATNA identified, RP and aspiration set, but didn't strengthen BATNA before entering)
- Positioning: 2 (anchored below aspiration, creating a ceiling too low)
- Flexibility: 2 (unilateral concession without conditional structure)
- Composure: 3 (delivered the ask, held through first pushback, but moved too fast on counter)
- Outcome: 2 (above RP but well below achievable range)

**Pattern match:** "This fits the pattern we've seen — your preparation is solid but your opening position undersells your own target. The aspiration-to-anchor gap keeps costing you."

## Example 4: MESO Package Construction

**Context:** Multi-issue vendor contract. User has built a scoring system:

| Issue | Weight | Walk-Away | Acceptable | Good | Best |
|-------|--------|-----------|------------|------|------|
| Monthly rate | 40 | 0 | 15 | 30 | 40 |
| Contract length | 25 | 0 | 10 | 18 | 25 |
| Payment terms | 20 | 0 | 8 | 14 | 20 |
| Scope flexibility | 15 | 0 | 6 | 10 | 15 |

RP: 39 points. Aspiration: 72 points.

**Three MESO packages (each scoring ~68 points):**

| Issue | Package A | Package B | Package C |
|-------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Monthly rate | Good (30) | Best (40) | Acceptable (15) |
| Contract length | Good (18) | Acceptable (10) | Best (25) |
| Payment terms | Acceptable (8) | Good (14) | Best (20) |
| Scope flexibility | Good (10) | Walk-Away (0) | Acceptable (6) |
| **Total** | **66** | **64** | **66** |

**Interpretation guide:**
- If they pick Package A: balanced priorities — they value consistency across terms
- If they pick Package B: rate is their top priority, willing to trade on length and scope
- If they pick Package C: long-term commitment and cash flow (payment terms) matter more than the headline rate — this suggests a budget-constrained counterparty who needs predictability

**Presentation language:**
"I've put together three options that all work for me — I'm genuinely flexible between them. [Present packages.] Which of these is closest to what works for you? Or is there a combination we should explore?"

## Example 5: Integrative vs. Distributive Misdiagnosis

**Scenario:** User is negotiating a salary raise with their current employer. They treated it as distributive (single-issue: salary number) and anchored aggressively at 25% above current.

**Analysis:**
"You diagnosed this as distributive — one number, zero-sum. But a raise negotiation with your current employer is almost always mixed. Yes, the salary number itself is distributive. But there are likely other issues on the table: title, responsibilities, equity/bonus, remote flexibility, professional development budget, review timeline.

By treating it as pure salary, you forced a distributive frame where the only question was 'how much more.' Your manager's BATNA is to say no and keep you at current salary — which is strong. But if you'd expanded the issue set, you could have offered trades: 'I'm flexible on timing if we can agree on the target number' or 'If the budget for base is constrained, could we structure a performance bonus that triggers at [metric]?'

The misdiagnosis narrowed your options. The fix: before your next conversation, list every issue that could be on the table — not just salary — and prioritize them. Then bring 2-3 packages to the conversation."
