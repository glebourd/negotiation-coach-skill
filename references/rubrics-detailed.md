# Detailed Scoring Rubric

## Five-Dimension Rubric

Used in `simulate`, `analyze`, `debrief`, and `progress`. All scores are 1-5, calibrated to the user's experience band (novice / developing / experienced).

---

### Preparation (1-5)

**What it measures:** How well the negotiator understood the landscape before entering — BATNA, RP, aspiration, ZOPA, counterparty interests, issues, and information strategy.

| Score | Novice | Developing | Experienced |
|-------|--------|------------|-------------|
| **1** | No BATNA identified. No sense of what to ask for. | Vague BATNA, no RP set, no counterparty analysis. | Entered with minimal preparation despite having the resources to do more. |
| **2** | Has a general sense of what they want but hasn't quantified it. | BATNA identified but not evaluated for strength. RP unquantified. | BATNA and RP set but no counterparty analysis. Missing hidden parties or timing dynamics. |
| **3** | BATNA identified. Aspiration stated. Basic issue awareness. | BATNA evaluated, RP quantified, aspiration set. Counterparty BATNA estimated. | Full BATNA/RP/ZOPA analysis. Counterparty interests mapped. Issues listed with priorities. |
| **4** | BATNA identified, issues listed, aspiration pushed higher than first instinct. | All of 3 plus: concession ladder drafted, information strategy planned, issues prioritized for both sides. | All of 3 plus: third-party intel, timing dynamics, coalition analysis, MESO packages or scoring system built. |
| **5** | Exceptional for experience level — thorough analysis with quantified targets and contingency plans. | Full preparation including MESO packages, counterparty BATNA weakening strategy, and practiced opening scripts. | Full landscape including competitive intelligence, relationship mapping, multi-round strategy. Preparation quality would impress a professional negotiator. |

---

### Positioning (1-5)

**What it measures:** How effectively the negotiator set the frame — opening anchor, justification, value articulation, and initial impression.

| Score | Novice | Developing | Experienced |
|-------|--------|------------|-------------|
| **1** | No anchor set. Waited for counterpart and accepted their frame. Or opened with RP. | Anchored near RP with no room to maneuver. Or apologized for the ask. | Anchor was unjustified or contradicted by available market data. |
| **2** | Set an anchor but couldn't justify it when challenged. | Anchored at aspiration (not above), limiting room for concessions. Justification was weak. | Anchor was reasonable but not strategically positioned. Frame was reactive to counterparty's opening. |
| **3** | Anchored above aspiration with a basic rationale. Held position through first pushback. | Anchored above aspiration with market data or precedent as justification. Maintained the frame through moderate pressure. | Strong anchor with multiple justification layers. Counterparty had to work to move the number. |
| **4** | Anchored aggressively with clear justification. Re-anchored when counterpart tried to set a different frame. | All of 3 plus: framing language was strategic (not adversarial), created a narrative the counterparty could work within. | Sophisticated anchoring with issue linkage — used the anchor on one issue to create movement on others. Frame was collaborative despite being aggressive. |
| **5** | Exceptional — set the entire negotiation's frame from the opening move. | The counterparty negotiated within the frame set by the user, not vice versa. Justification was compelling and hard to counter. | Masterful positioning — the counterparty felt they were in a fair negotiation while the frame consistently favored the user. |

---

### Flexibility (1-5)

**What it measures:** Strategic adaptation — concession quality, issue trades, ability to expand value, and knowing what to hold vs. give.

| Score | Novice | Developing | Experienced |
|-------|--------|------------|-------------|
| **1** | No concessions made (rigid) or all concessions given away immediately (no discipline). | Concessions were unilateral — gave without getting. Or held position on everything and lost the deal. | Conceded on high-priority items while holding low-priority ones. Opposite of strategic. |
| **2** | Made concessions but not conditional. Didn't ask for anything in return. | Concessions were roughly conditional but not shrinking in size. Gave too much too fast. | Concessions were conditional but didn't exploit issue differences — missed obvious trades. |
| **3** | Conditional concessions. Some attempt to trade across issues. | Conditional, shrinking concessions. Made at least one value-creating trade. | Systematic concession ladder followed. Multiple trades executed. Expanded the issue set when appropriate. |
| **4** | Conditional, shrinking concessions with clear trades. Identified at least one issue the counterpart valued more than they did. | All of 3 plus: introduced new issues to create trading room. Concessions were labeled and justified. | MESOs used or could have been. Concession ladder adapted in real-time based on counterpart signals. Creative trades that expanded total value. |
| **5** | Exceptional — turned a seemingly single-issue negotiation into a multi-issue trade. | Full concession discipline with real-time adaptation. The counterpart felt they were getting value while the user achieved their priorities. | The deal created more total value than either side expected. Flexibility was strategic, not accommodating — every concession served a purpose. |

---

### Composure (1-5)

**What it measures:** Emotional regulation, silence tolerance, pressure handling, and avoidance of reactive decisions.

| Score | Novice | Developing | Experienced |
|-------|--------|------------|-------------|
| **1** | Folded immediately under any pressure. Accepted terms without negotiating. Or became visibly frustrated and damaged the relationship. | Filled every silence within 3 seconds. Made reactive concessions under pressure. Apologized for negotiating. | Lost composure in a way that was visible to the counterparty. Made a decision driven by emotion rather than strategy. |
| **2** | Held briefly but conceded when pressure continued. Filled silence with unnecessary justification that weakened position. | Held silence for 5-7 seconds but cracked. Responded to emotional pressure with a concession instead of a question. | Maintained surface composure but made strategic errors driven by impatience or anxiety (e.g., closing too early to end the discomfort). |
| **3** | Held position through first pushback. Tolerated moderate silence (7-10 seconds). Didn't apologize for asking. | Held silence for 10+ seconds. Responded to pressure with questions, not concessions. Took a break when needed. | Full emotional regulation under pressure. Used silence strategically. Counterparty's aggression or flattery didn't affect decisions. |
| **4** | Held through sustained pressure. Used silence intentionally. Asked for breaks when needed without appearing weak. | All of 3 plus: actually used the counterparty's emotional pressure as information about their position ("They're getting aggressive because they need this deal"). | Used composure as a tool — the stillness itself communicated confidence. Pressure from the counterparty was met with calm curiosity, not defensiveness. |
| **5** | Exceptional poise for experience level — stayed strategic throughout despite genuine stakes. | The counterparty's pressure tactics were ineffective. The user's composure was itself a source of leverage. | Complete mastery — emotional regulation was invisible. The negotiation felt calm and productive despite genuine tension. |

---

### Outcome (1-5)

**What it measures:** Whether the negotiation achieved the user's objectives relative to their RP and aspiration, and whether the process was sustainable (relationship preserved, principles upheld).

| Score | Novice | Developing | Experienced |
|-------|--------|------------|-------------|
| **1** | Accepted a deal below RP or walked away when a deal above RP was available. | Deal was at or below RP with no compensating non-monetary terms. Or destroyed a relationship that mattered. | Below RP, or at RP in a situation where the ZOPA was wide and more was available. Or violated a core principle (revealed RP, bluffed BATNA that was called). |
| **2** | Deal was at RP — barely acceptable. No value creation. | Deal was between RP and aspiration but closer to RP. Value was left on the table through missed trades. | Above RP but well below what preparation and leverage should have achieved. |
| **3** | Deal was above RP. Basic objectives met. | Deal was near aspiration. Key interests addressed. No principle violations. | Aspiration achieved or exceeded on primary issues. Relationship intact. Process was professional. |
| **4** | Deal was near aspiration. The user felt good about the outcome. | Aspiration achieved or exceeded. Multiple issues resolved favorably. Relationship strengthened or maintained. | Outcome exceeded preparation targets. Value was created that neither side initially expected. Process positioned the user well for future interactions. |
| **5** | Exceptional outcome — above what the user thought was possible, achieved through real skill rather than luck. | Outcome at or above the top of the estimated ZOPA. The counterparty felt the deal was fair despite the user claiming significant value. | A deal that a professional negotiator would be proud of — maximized value, preserved relationship, and positioned for future leverage. |

## Root Cause Taxonomy

When a dimension scores low, don't just name the gap — diagnose the root cause.

| Pattern | Likely Root Cause | Coaching Response |
|---|---|---|
| Low Preparation despite time to prepare | User doesn't know what preparation looks like | Teach the `plan` framework — BATNA, RP, aspiration, ZOPA, issues |
| Low Preparation despite knowing the framework | Avoidance — preparation makes the negotiation feel "real" | Address psychological readiness; reframe preparation as confidence-building, not anxiety-producing |
| Low Positioning despite strong preparation | Fear of seeming aggressive or greedy | Reframe: "Anchoring high is not greedy — it's strategic. The counterparty expects it." |
| Low Positioning — anchor too close to RP | User conflates aspiration with anchor | Teach the difference: aspiration is your goal; anchor is above your goal to create room |
| Low Flexibility — rigid, no trades | User sees negotiation as single-issue | Teach issue expansion; run `score` to quantify multi-issue dynamics |
| Low Flexibility — gave everything away | User is conflict-averse and sees concession as relationship-building | Reframe: "Unconditional concessions don't build relationships — they signal weakness. Conditional concessions signal respect." |
| Low Composure — folds under silence | Interprets silence as rejection | Drill with `simulate silence`; reframe silence as processing |
| Low Composure — reactive under pressure | Emotional flooding — amygdala overrides strategy | Coach pre-negotiation calming routines; build break-taking as a habit |
| Low Composure despite preparation | The gap isn't knowledge — it's state management | Focus on `simulate` under progressively higher pressure layers |
| Low Outcome despite good process | Bad luck, bad information, or misread counterparty BATNA | Separate process quality from outcome. A good process that produces a bad outcome is still a good process. |
| Low Outcome — accepted too quickly | Relief and gratitude override strategy at the finish line | Teach the "one more ask" discipline: never accept without at least one additional request |
| All dimensions moderate (3s), no dimension strong | Jack of all trades, master of none | Focus on one dimension to push to 4+; depth before breadth |

## Self-Assessment Delta Interpretation

| Pattern | What It Means | Coaching Adjustment |
|---|---|---|
| User consistently rates higher than coach | Blind spots — user doesn't see what's not working | Be more direct in feedback. Show specific moments that justify the lower score. |
| User consistently rates lower than coach | Confidence gap — performing better than they believe | Name the gap: "You're scoring yourself lower than I would. That self-doubt may be costing you more than any skill gap." |
| Delta varies by dimension | Selective awareness — strong self-read on some skills, blind on others | Focus on the dimensions where the delta is largest |
| Delta shrinks over time | Calibration improving — this is a key coaching outcome | Acknowledge it: "Your self-assessment is getting more accurate. That's one of the most important skills in negotiation." |
| Coach scores suspiciously always match user | Coach is anchoring to user's self-assessment | Reset: score the next session before asking for self-assessment |
